How to Build a Gaming PC

Find out how to build a gaming PC with Crucial. From the parts you need, to building a gaming PC on a budget, check it out now on our website.

When you build a gaming PC to save money, make sure you at least match the minimum system requirements of the games you want to play. But if your budget has room, it’s wise to account for the rising system requirements of games you will want in the future. Completing a build only to run out of space a few months later will likely be more frustrating than being killed over and over by someone camped out in your favorite first person shooter game.

So, what do you need to build a gaming PC? Hardware is the engine of your gaming rig. It accounts for almost all the performance available– that includes the speed, responsiveness, frame rates, and running multiple apps at one time (for example, gaming while recording your gameplay on video) – other accessories and software can help and are still important, but they’re complementary and their impact is dictated by the capabilities of the hardware.

When you put all the parts and tools together, make sure you have plenty of room to keep your build organized. Be aware of static electricity as you build – it’s one of the few ways the hardware can be damaged but it’s easy to avoid. Frequently ground yourself by touching an unpainted metal surface or wear an electrostatic discharge (ESD) wrist strap to protect your system’s components from the static electricity that’s naturally present in your body. It’s also helpful to keep a can of compressed air to remove any dust or fine debris from the interface as you’re installing the processor, memory, and SSD. For instructions on installing the processor, power supply, and putting the motherboard in the case, consult each component’s owner’s manual. The act of installation or assembling parts isn’t complicated, but there is the potential for errors. That’s why it’s best to follow the more detailed step-by-step instructions for each specific part

Step 1: Motherboard

Lay your motherboard out at your workstation.

Step 2: Install the CPU

Remove the CPU from its packaging, do not touch the gold pins on the bottom. Install your processor onto the motherboard by pressing firmly and following the manufacturers recommendations. Apply a pea sized amount of thermal paste if installing a heatsink. Secure the heatsink over the CPU with the 4 screws.

Step 3: Install the RAM

RAM is the easiest hardware to install when you’re building a PC. Locate the memory slots on the motherboard. Hold your memory modules on the side to avoid touching the gold pins. If your memory doesn’t have a heat spreader, also avoid touching the chips on the module. Align the notches on the module with the ridge in the slot then firmly press the module in until it clicks. As you’re pressing, note that it takes about 30 pounds of pressure to fully install a module.

Step 4: Install the motherboard in the case

Install the I/O shield into the rectangular opening of the case. Next, slide the motherboard into the case until the ports fit through the I/O shield. Once installed, secure the motherboard with the included screws into the case. Do not overtighten, this can lead to cracking on the motherboard.

Step 5: Install the GPU

Slide your GPU into its slot and press until it clicks, secure with any included screws.

Step 6: Install the SSD

Depending on the form factor of the SSD you’ve purchased (2.5-inch, mSATA, or M.2), installation requires attaching the drive to the storage interface, then fitting it into the drive bay (if it’s a 2.5-inch SSD).

Step 7: Install the fans

Install your cooling fan with any included mounting brackets. For CPU fans, ensure constant downward pressure is made with the CPU when the cam lever engages with the mounting bracket. Plug the fans into the motherboard.

Step 8: Install the power supply

Remove the power supply retaining bracket. Attach the bracket to the power supply, slide the power supply into the case until it is flush with the case.

Step 9: Attach the appropriate cables

Attach all USB, audio, GPU, CPU, drives, power supply, heatsink, and cooling cables per the manufacturer’s instructions.

Step 10: Plug in your peripherals

Plug in your mouse, keyboard, monitor, and headset if you have one.

Step 11: Flip the switch!

When your system is assembled, it’s time to hit the power button! Hopefully, everything should turn on.

Step 12: Time to boot up!

If everything worked correctly, a screen will appear where you can enter the system BIOS. If you have a disc or flash drive with an OS, put it into the appropriate drive, boot up, and install the OS.

The Best Computer Specs for Gaming

Building a gaming PC but don't know how to get the best for your favorite games? Get the best performance for your PC and gaming spec guide.

Your basic setup should contain the following:

Central processing unit (CPU), Motherboard, CPU cooler, Random access memory (RAM), Graphics processing unit (GPU), Hard drive (HDD) or solid state drive (SSD) , Peripherals (mouse, monitor, keyboard)

Compatibility is your number one priority! Remember, checking the socket isn’t always enough. Before buying the latest processor, make sure it is compatible with the motherboard you've chosen. Also, not having the right CPU can affect the speed of your memory as in limiting it’s performance.

Many new AMD® and Intel® processors feed on high-speed DDR4 memory. The more memory speed the processor has to work with, the higher its output. As a result, you get a faster, more responsive rig. Remember, you're memory must be compatible with your CPU and motherboard.
